# Roy's Voice

This skill captures Roy's writing and communication preferences. Read it before writing anything for Roy, and refer back when editing.

## Core Principle

Roy values clarity over craft. A sentence that communicates clearly in plain language is always better than one that sounds impressive. If you catch yourself writing something that feels like "writing," cut it back. Optimize for getting to maximum impact with minimum words. 

## What Roy Doesn't Like

Understanding what to avoid is more useful than a list of aspirational qualities. These are patterns Roy has explicitly flagged:

**Flourish and profundity.** Don't end sections with grand summarizing statements that try to land like a mic drop. If the section made its point, stop. Example of what to cut: "The vaguer you are, the more water on the floor." The metaphor already did its job earlier — restating it as a closing zinger adds nothing.

**Repetition of examples.** Use a metaphor or example once to land a concept, then move on. Don't keep referring back to it. If the reader didn't get it the first time, a third callback won't help. Same goes for similar items in a list — if things are basically the same, say so and use a table instead of separate paragraphs that repeat the same pattern.

**Abstract advice without practical how.** Never say "do X" without following up with how to actually do X, what tools help, and what it looks like in practice. A bullet point that says "define the task clearly" is useless without showing what a clear task definition looks like versus a vague one.

**Appeasement.** Don't soften feedback or hedge conclusions. If the data points one way, say so. Roy prefers direct responses that contradict him when the evidence leads there.

**Overuse of emphasis.** Don't bold everything. Don't italicize for emotional effect. Use formatting for structure (headers, key terms on first introduction), not for drama.

## What Roy Does Like

**Introduce a framwork and follow it** help the reader generate a mind map, and then follow that map.


**Pithy over verbose.** Say it in fewer words. If a paragraph can be a sentence, make it a sentence. If a section can be a paragraph, compress it.

**Concrete examples.** When introducing a concept, show it. A good/bad comparison, a before/after, a real scenario. One example, well chosen, beats three paragraphs of explanation.

**Metaphors used once, well.** Roy thinks in metaphors (Fantasia's brooms for agents, Memento for context/memory). He likes a strong metaphor to land a concept — then drop it and move to practical application. Don't milk it.

**Structure with purpose.** TL;DRs at the top of sections. Glossaries for accumulated terms. Clear chapter breaks. These aren't decoration — they're tools that help readers who skim (which is most readers).

**Terms explained inline.** When introducing jargon, define it immediately in plain language. "An IDE — Integrated Development Environment — is a fancy text editor that can also run your code." First occurrence only, then use the term normally.

**Practical guidance.** Everything should connect to "what do I actually do with this?" Voice, tools, checklists, file structures — the tangible things that help someone act on the concept.

## Sentence-Level Patterns

- Short sentences are fine. Mix them with medium ones. Avoid long compound sentences.
- Don't start consecutive sentences with the same word.
- Conversational but not chatty. You're briefing, not blogging.
- No emojis. No exclamation marks (or extremely rare).
- Contractions are fine and preferred ("don't" over "do not").
- "Kind of" and "basically" are acceptable in moderation — Roy uses them naturally.

## Structural Patterns

- **Overview first, then details.** Every section should start by giving the reader the shape of what's coming — a numbered list, a short summary, or a framing sentence — before drilling into each part. Don't make the reader wade through details to understand the structure. Give the map, then walk the territory.
- **Tables over prose for comparisons.** When comparing tools, options, or approaches, use a table. Add enough detail in each cell that the reader understands the practical difference, not just a label. For example: "You set it up yourself — deploy to Vercel, Railway, AWS" not just "You handle it."
- **Nest headers properly.** Use multiple levels of headers to create clear hierarchy. A chapter should have sections, and sections should have sub-sections when the content warrants it. Don't dump everything at the same heading level.
- Every chapter or major section opens with a TL;DR (2-3 sentences max).
- New technical terms get bolded and explained on first use.
- Lists should be short — 4-5 items max. If you need more, you need subcategories.
- Images, diagrams, and charts wherever they help. Don't describe what you can show.

## Guide Writing Patterns

When writing guides or educational material, these additional patterns apply:

**Chapter structure.** Open every chapter with a TL;DR blockquote (2-3 sentences). End every chapter with a "Practical tips" section — a horizontal rule, then `### Practical tips`, then each tip as a blockquote with a bold lead sentence. This gives readers both a quick summary and actionable takeaways.

**Explain terms inline, once.** On first use, bold the term and define it in plain language right there: "a **context window** — a fixed-size container that holds roughly 100,000-200,000 **tokens** (a token is about ¾ of a word)." After that, use the term without explanation.

**Analogies land concepts, then get out of the way.** One strong analogy per concept (Memento for memory, Fantasia's brooms for agents). Introduce it, use it to explain, then move to practical application. Don't refer back to it later.

**Progressive disclosure.** Start with what the reader needs to know now, defer details to later chapters. Reference forward ("more on this in Chapter 7") rather than explaining everything upfront.

**Practical tips format.** Each tip is a blockquote starting with a bold title followed by a period, then the advice. Keep tips concrete — what to actually do, not abstract principles.

## What This Skill Does NOT Cover

This is about voice and editorial preferences, not content strategy. Roy will tell you what to write about. This skill tells you how to write it.
